Module 3: Natural Language Processing (NLP)

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is the intersection of artificial intelligence and linguistics. It enables computers to read, interpret, analyze, and generate human language in a way that is both meaningful and contextually relevant.

The Evolution of NLP Techniques

NLP has transitioned from rule-based systems to deep learning models that capture the nuances of human speech.

  • Preprocessing & Tokenization: Breaking down text into tokens, lemmatization (base forms), and removing stop-words.
  • Vectorization (Word Embeddings): Mapping words into numerical high-dimensional space (e.g., Word2Vec) where similar meanings are close together.
  • Sequence Modeling: Older models like RNNs and LSTMs were the first to handle context, though they struggled with long-range dependencies.

The Transformer Revolution

The current state-of-the-art is dominated by the Transformer architecture, which uses Self-Attention.

  • Self-Attention: Allows the model to look at every word in a sentence simultaneously to understand its importance.
  • Contextual Understanding: Transformers can easily distinguish meanings, like identifying if "bank" refers to a river or a financial institution based on surrounding words.

Core NLP Tasks

Modern models are trained for complex functions:

  • Sentiment Analysis: Is the text positive or negative?
  • Machine Translation: Converting languages accurately.
  • NER: Identifying names, places, and dates.
  • Summarization: Condensing long documents.

Key Concepts

Large Language Models (LLMs): Massive models trained on the internet to predict the next word with fluency.

Context Window: The amount of text a model can "remember" at one time.

Fine-tuning: Training a pre-trained model on a specific domain (like law or medicine) to make it an expert.
